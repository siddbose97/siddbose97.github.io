Keeping Soldiers out of Harm's Way

The ODD Feedback Bot

Every branch of the military has one thing in common: safety is paramount. From an organizational standpoint, a breach in safety protocols is equivalent to mission failure. Although this is a well-meaning intention, at the end of the day safety is in the hands of the soldiers on the ground. Thus emerges a problem: how can we ensure that soldiers are being safe even if it is tedious. For many years, the returning of weapons has signaled the end of a long day's work for soldiers around the world. In the Singapore Armed Forces, however, one obstacle stands between the soldiers and their freedom: a notebook. This notebook is each battalion's log of ODD's or Operator Detected Defects. These are issues with the weapons the soldiers notice on any given day and are supposed to be sent over to the 6th Army Maintenance Battalion where I am stationed. Neglecting to fill out the notebook in a timely manner not only increases repair times but is the leading cause of potentially lethal incidents during training. As such, I set out to devise a way for soldiers to be able to report the issues in parallel in less than 30 seconds.


In the end, I settled on leveraging Telegram's bot feature due to its popularity in Singapore. Two issues stood in my way: a lack of funding and a tepid level of enthusiasm among my superior officers. The former required the biggest workaround. Although Telegram makes it relatively simple to instantiate a bot in their environment, hosting the bot is the developer's challenge. Without any funding, I scoured the web for a free option and found out that Heroku would host the bot for free for 1000 hours a month (sounds like overkill but I'm assuming running multiple bots would divide that allocation). Although the free server was somewhat tedious to work with and often slow to update, in the end, the bot was deployed.


The initial unenthusiastic response to the bot's development soon turned into intrigue and later relief. Like many projects, the proof was indeed in the pudding. In just 3 months after deployment, the ODD feedback levels had risen by over 700% and over 150 of the defects were marked "major". A major defect is one that can in the best case cause severe injury but often spells much worse for the soldiers. Knowing that my bot is able to prevent these incident makes the hours of software development and butting heads with my superior officers worth it.
