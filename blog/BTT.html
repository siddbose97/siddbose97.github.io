National Service Chapter 2: Technician Training Begins

August 15, 2021

Hormat SAF!

I grew up moving countries every few years but even compared to that transient life the 9 months between June 2019 and April 2020 gave me whiplash. I went from graduating from UCLA in June as an Indian Citizen to starting my first job in November as a Singaporean Citizen to enlisting in the army in April of the following year. The change in scenery, accompanied by the plague of Covid-19 taking over the world made for a jarring experience that took months to fully comprehend and eventually appreciate. Today, on August 9th, 2021 I am 8 months away from my Operationally Ready Date (ORD), the day I will rise from the ashes as a civilian phoenix once again. Theatrics aside, I have never faced as many challenges, hurdles, and disappointments as I have endured during my time in the army but have also learned so much along the way. This blog post is both an ode to National Service (NS) and the telling of a classic Singaporean story told by an outsider.

Chapter 1: Basic Military Training (BMT)

April 2nd, 2020 was my official enlistment date, but due to the quarantine order I was placed under when I flew into Singapore in late March and the pandemic picking up pace, the first month of my BMT was unorthodox, to say the least. With Singapore in a "Circuit Breaker", the country was essentially shut down. My days started early, with first parade (FP) being conducted over WhatsApp. What proceeded was an ad hoc blend of Google Hangout-led fitness programs and a plethora of slide shows detailing what it meant to be a soldier in Singapore. Between the marching commands being given in Malay and a horde of protocols vis-a-vis dress codes, greetings, and general etiquette, not much stuck. 

Then came May 26th, the fateful day that I went to Pulau Tekong, an island off the coast of Singapore, home to the Basic Military Training Center (BMTC) and my home for the next six weeks. Confinement, as it was known, refers to the first few weeks where you stay in camp through weekends. Typical enlistees are confined for two weeks, but with Covid cutting training times short, our confinement was going to be three weeks long. Now this may sound relatively benign, but once you realize that phone chargers are prohibited, the challenges begin to mount (suffice it to say, the power bank business in Singapore is booming)!


The first memory I have in BMT was from my first night of confinement. Our section commanders, 3rd Sergeants, were yelling about something or the other and ended with the classic, “Do you understand????”. Now I need to be honest. My ears were not yet accustomed to the Singaporean accent, and my naivete kicked in. The fact was, I had no idea what was said to us preceding the question, so I just said “No!”. Great first impression to set. 


The highlights and lowlights came thick and fast after the first night. On the third day we were issued our SAR21s (Singapore Assault Rifle 21st Century) and by the end of week 2 I was missing Coca Cola just as much as I missed the mainland. High-Key (HK) activities are by far the most challenging during BMT, and passing every one of them is mandatory to graduate. In a two week period we completed every HK from the Basic Trainfire Package (a 100m shooting range test), Close Quarter Battle (urban warfare) to the dreaded field camp. Although I left field camp with unspeakable heat rashes (I couldn’t walk around for days lest my skin feel like it was taken over by fire ants), I must say I learned a few things for sure. 


During a drill called “Fire Movement” where we mimic jungle warfare, shooting at fake enemies in a heavily forested area, we were forced to lie in a prone position to reduce our exposure. Now Singapore being Singapore, we were blessed with all the rain Toto could ever ask for a few hours earlier. Looking down at the mud (and no you haven’t seen mud till your boots are 4 inches deep in the ground), the clean freak inside me shuddered. Two seconds later my face was in the mud. This experience has shaped my life: whenever there’s a task to complete, be it refactoring someone’s awful code or even grabbing the TV remote at home, I’m inclined to do it with no questions asked. It will always be better than being 3 inches deep in the mud with the grass tickling my nose. A couple of tarantulas in my bag and bull frog mating calls later, field camp was over. 


The last step in BMT has historically been the Passing Out Parade (POP), which usually takes place in front of the beautiful Marina Bay Sands. Unfortunately, due to Covid, not only was the venue shifted to Tekong itself, no guests were allowed. In the end we graduated with our platoon mates and by the end of the day we watched Tekong fade into the background as we took the last ferry back to the mainland. 


BMT, in hindsight, was one of the most gruelling and intense experiences of my life but I couldn’t be more thankful for it. Waking up at 5am every day and being berated by commanders for missing a crease on our bed sheets or a speck of dust on the floor (the bunks are not air conditioned so obviously there is dust coming in…) is something I will never miss, but the brotherhood formed in our platoons as we suffered and learned together will be cherished forever. After POP we had a week off which is when we found out what our vocations would be.
